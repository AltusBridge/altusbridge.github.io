GIFT City vs Mauritius vs DIFC — A Practitioner's Comparison

Abhishek Mohta Altus Bridge Advisory Fund Structuring · Cross-Border Capital

Choosing the right fund domicile is one of the most consequential decisions for any India-focused fund. GIFT City, Mauritius, and DIFC each offer distinct regulatory, tax, and investor access advantages — and the right answer depends entirely on your capital source, investor profile, and deployment strategy.

There is no universally correct answer to the GIFT City vs Mauritius vs DIFC question. Each jurisdiction has evolved to serve a specific type of capital flow, investor base, and structuring need. What follows is a practitioner-level comparison drawn from hands-on experience structuring funds across all three.

GIFT City — India's domestic IFC

GIFT City (Gujarat International Finance Tec-City) is India's first and only onshore International Financial Services Centre, regulated by IFSCA. It sits outside Indian domestic tax law for most purposes, operating as a USD-denominated financial zone within India.

Key strengths

  • Onshore access: Direct access to Indian capital markets and SEBI-regulated instruments without the friction of cross-border remittance
  • IBU banking: IFSC Banking Units offer USD lending, trade finance, and ECB structuring that is unavailable domestically
  • FME framework: The Fund Management Entity framework allows non-retail fund management with lighter regulation than full SEBI AIF registration
  • NISM / IFSCA licensing: Streamlined licensing for investment advisers and fund managers under the 2026 draft regulations
  • Tax pass-through: AIF Category I/II at GIFT City enjoy pass-through tax treatment — the fund itself is not taxed, investors are
  • Rupee / USD dual operation: Unique ability to operate both INR and USD books within the same structure

Practitioner note: GIFT City works best when your LP base is predominantly Indian — resident HNIs, family offices, corporates, or NRIs investing under LRS. For GCC or European institutional capital, the combination of GIFT City (SPV / deployment vehicle) + offshore fund (Mauritius / DIFC) as a feeder is often more efficient.

Watch points

  • Ecosystem is still maturing — fund administration, legal, and audit depth is thinner than Mauritius or DIFC
  • Foreign institutional investors may still prefer offshore entry for familiarity and DTAA certainty
  • IFSCA regulations are evolving rapidly — the 2026 Financial Advisers draft regulations introduce new licensing requirements

Mauritius — the established India gateway

Mauritius has been the dominant offshore entry point for India-focused capital for over three decades. Post the 2016 DTAA renegotiation, the original capital gains exemption was phased out, but Mauritius retains significant structural and operational advantages.

Key strengths

  • Established ecosystem: Deep pool of fund administrators, lawyers, auditors, and banks with India expertise
  • VCC framework: Variable Capital Company structure allows legal segregation between sub-funds — critical for project finance lenders who require ring-fenced security
  • GBL Category 1: Global Business Licence companies retain treaty access for dividends, interest, and royalties even post-2016
  • Africa + Asia connectivity: Mauritius DTAAs cover 46 countries — useful for funds with Sub-Saharan Africa or ASEAN co-investment mandates
  • Singapore–Mauritius corridor: Singapore family offices and institutions are highly familiar and comfortable with Mauritius structures

Practitioner note: For project finance-linked fund structures where lenders require legal segregation between sub-fund assets and liabilities, the Mauritius VCC remains the cleanest solution available. A Mauritius VCC + Indian AIF master-feeder is a well-tested and lender-accepted structure.

Watch points

  • Capital gains on Indian equities / listed securities are now taxed at source in India post-2016 renegotiation
  • FATF grey-listing in 2021 (since removed) heightened KYC and compliance burden — still adds friction for some LPs
  • Substance requirements have increased — a nominal registered office is no longer sufficient

DIFC — the West Asia capital gateway

The Dubai International Financial Centre is the premier financial hub for GCC and broader West Asia capital. DFSA regulation is robust, internationally recognised, and gives access to a deep pool of UHNW, family office, and sovereign wealth capital in the region.

Key strengths

  • GCC investor access: The natural domicile choice when your primary LP base is in UAE, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Qatar, or Bahrain
  • DFSA credibility: DFSA-regulated fund managers command significantly higher trust with institutional and sovereign investors than offshore-light structures
  • Qualified Investor Fund (QIF): Light-touch, fast-setup fund vehicle for sophisticated investors — no prospectus requirement, 15-day registration
  • Co-investment SPVs: DIFC is highly efficient for deal-by-deal co-investment structures alongside a master fund
  • Nasdaq Dubai listing: Access to regional capital markets and sukuk structuring for Islamic finance-compliant vehicles
  • India–UAE DTAA: Post-2016 India-UAE CEPA and the bilateral investment treaty provide a framework for direct India investment with treaty protection

Practitioner note: DIFC is the right choice when you are specifically targeting GCC capital and need regulatory credibility in front of sovereign funds or large regional family offices. It is not the most efficient pure tax structure for India deployment, but it is unmatched for LP origination and relationship capital in West Asia.

Watch points

  • Higher substance requirements — DFSA expects genuine presence, not a brass-plate office
  • Higher cost of setup and ongoing compliance versus Mauritius or GIFT City
  • India DTAA benefits are limited — pure tax optimisation via DIFC is less effective than Mauritius for certain asset classes

When to use which — decision framework

Use GIFT City when

GIFT City

Your LPs are Indian residents, NRIs under LRS, or domestic family offices. You want onshore USD banking (IBU). You are building an India-only deployment mandate. You want to combine SEBI AIF with an IFSCA FME in a master-feeder.

Use Mauritius when

Mauritius

Your LPs are Singapore, European, or African institutions. You need legal sub-fund segregation for project finance lenders (VCC). You want an established, lender-familiar offshore jurisdiction with deep service provider depth.

Use DIFC when

DIFC

Your LPs are GCC family offices, sovereign funds, or UHNW individuals. You need DFSA-regulated credibility to open doors. You are raising from West Asia and routing capital into India or broader MENA + India mandates.

The hybrid approach

In practice, the most sophisticated India-focused fund structures combine two or three jurisdictions in a master-feeder or parallel fund architecture:

  • DIFC feeder → Mauritius master → India AIF: GCC capital enters via DIFC, pools at Mauritius master, deploys into India through SEBI AIF — combining LP access, legal segregation, and regulatory familiarity
  • GIFT City FME + Mauritius SPV: Domestic management entity at GIFT City, offshore SPV in Mauritius for international co-investors, single India deployment vehicle
  • DIFC QIF + GIFT City IBU: Deal-by-deal co-investment via DIFC QIF, USD debt financing via GIFT City IBU, combined for infrastructure transactions requiring both equity and debt tranches

Bottom line: The jurisdiction decision is downstream of the LP question. Start with who is your investor? — their nationality, tax residence, regulatory comfort, and existing relationships will point you to the right domicile faster than any tax table.
